CORRECTION AND ADDITIONAL DETAIL TO THE ORIGINAL REPORT

Re-examining the logs after filing, I found two errors in my original submission
and one omission that materially changes the reproduction steps. Corrected
details below; the full corrected body is attached as
0-full-description-CORRECTED-2026-07-20.txt.


CORRECTION 1 - Incident 1 did not stay on the built-in speakers

I wrote that incident 1 targeted device 100 (BuiltInSpeakerDevice). That is
wrong. Within the single runaway, ControlCenter thrashes across THREE CoreAudio
device IDs:

  DeviceID 198 (115 writes) -> 100 (26) -> 198 (920) -> 100 (57)
    -> 212 (404) -> 100 (7,160)

Window totals: 14,533 writes to device 100, 2,182 to 198, 865 to 212.

Critically, 198 and 212 are THE SAME physical device - UID C4-B3-49-AA-E7-97, a
pair of AirPods 4 - under two IDs, because they disconnected and reconnected
mid-runaway and were reassigned a new CoreAudio device ID.

This strengthens rather than weakens the race argument, and replaces my original
evidence point (b): a per-device state machine driven by real input would not
migrate between outputs mid-runaway, and would not follow the same physical
device across an ID change.


CORRECTION 2 - the severity is not theoretical

I described the hearing-exposure risk in the abstract. It was not abstract. The
affected output in incident 1 was the AirPods 4 identified above, which I was
wearing at the time. ControlCenter issued 1,158 writes setting that device to
volume 1.000000 - full scale, in-ear, reached in roughly 0.5 seconds with no
ramp.

Recovery required killing the agent from a terminal, which is not something a
user can do while the sound is painful.


OMISSION - a manual volume adjustment is part of the trigger

My original Steps to Reproduce jumped from the Spotify Connect transfer straight
to the runaway. The actual sequence had a step in between that I did not report:

  1. Spotify playing on an iPhone.
  2. In the Spotify desktop app on the Mac, switch playback to "This Computer".
  3. Adjust the system volume immediately afterwards - reflexively, in my case.
  4. Runaway.

The timing appears to matter:

  10:39:26.646  coreaudiod    >>> NEGOTIATE [com.spotify.client]
  10:39:26.835  ControlCenter (DeviceID 198) setLevel: main volume 0.500000
  10:39:26.849  ControlCenter set system volume: 0.435 -> 0.5   <-- +14 ms

No volume write of any kind appears in the log between 10:39:20 and
10:39:26.835, so the adjustment at .835 is the first mutation in the sequence.
It lands ~190 ms after the device negotiation - inside ControlCenter's
post-transfer volume re-sync. That collision is the most plausible source of the
two concurrent mutation paths the race needs.

Caveat: the log cannot distinguish a user-initiated setLevel from
ControlCenter's own re-sync, since both log identically. I offer this as
matching what I did and being consistent with the ordering, not as proof.


WITHDRAWN - original evidence point (d)

I originally claimed the non-1/16 values (0.435000, 0.490000) were "values
observed by one thread while another was mid-write". I am withdrawing that as
stated. They do appear only on the read side of the ratchet, which is consistent
with a lost update - but 0.435 could equally be the AirPods' own pre-existing
level when the transfer landed. I cannot separate the two from the log, so it
should not be counted as evidence. Points (a) through (c) carry the argument
without it.
